The Bell Family Story

Two Centuries, Five Hundred Miles

Drawn from Mary Wiltse Heck's 1985 family history and the research that followed it.

Out of Ulster, into the Backcountry

The Bells were Scots-Irish Presbyterians — part of the great wave of Ulster families who crossed the Atlantic in the 1700s and pushed into the Virginia backcountry. James Bell was born in Ohio County, Virginia, in 1776, the year of Independence. Around 1810 he brought his family over the mountains into the new state of Ohio and entered 320 acres of federal land in Liberty Township, Guernsey County — a patent signed under President James Madison. Joseph Bell and Margaret Hosack, and the Tigners of Morgan County, made the same westward journey in the same years.

Guernsey County, Ohio

For two generations the Bells farmed the hills around Cambridge. Hamilton Bell — born in Virginia in 1809, carried to Ohio as a baby — married his neighbor Agnes Bell, Joseph's daughter, binding the two Bell households into one line. Their son James Hamilton Bell (1832–1921) married Mary Elizabeth Tigner of Morgan County on September 20, 1851 — he was 19, she was 15, and the Morgan Herald printed the notice the following Friday. They are the central couple of this whole story, and of the 1985 book this record is built from.

Eleven Children, and the Road to Iowa

James Hamilton and Mary Elizabeth raised eleven children to adulthood — Jane, Agnes, Margaret, George, Ham, Ed, Will, Mollie, Charlie, Joe, and Milt. In 1890 the family joined the last great land migration, west to Iowa — first Story County, then the farms around West Bend, Rodman, and Oskaloosa. The book's pages are thick with their world — threshing crews and pheasant hunts, a racehorse that wouldn't race, church suppers, hard winters, and a family that kept showing up for each other across five hundred miles of prairie.

The Book Behind This Record

In the late 1970s, Mary Wiltse Heck — a great-granddaughter of the central couple — began eight years of letters, courthouse visits, and interviews with the oldest Bells then living. In September 1985 she typed up the result: The Bell Family History, 104 pages plus original wills, licenses, maps, and photographs. Every person, date, and story here comes from her work, page by page. It is the reason this family's first two centuries survive at all.

The Missing Name

The book carried one silence it did not know about. On its page for Joseph Merle Bell — grandson of the central couple through their tenth child, Joseph Franklin “Joe” Bell of West Bend — it lists five children, ending in 1937. But Joseph Merle and his wife Dorothy Walker had a sixth: Curtis Keith Bell, born February 8, 1940, in Plover, Iowa, ten miles from West Bend. His father died in 1957, when Curtis was seventeen, and somewhere in the years that followed the West Bend thread frayed — his name never reached the book's author. His mother Dorothy, born in Rodman in 1907, lived quietly on to 103, resting now in the same West Bend cemetery as four generations of this family. Curtis served nine years in the Navy and three in the Army in Vietnam, spent a career as a federal fire protection engineer, and died in 2021 of an illness the government ruled was caused by his service.

His son Landon Keith Bell — born in 1985, the year the book went to print — found his father's missing branch in 2026, matching the parents named on Curtis's death certificate to the book's own pages. The ★ marks trace that direct line, from James Bell of 1776 down to Landon, his wife Katharine, and their son — the ninth generation, arriving in 2026.

The Next Chapter

The book ends in 1985. Everyone born since — and every memory still carried by the generation that knew these farms — is the missing final section. That part can't be researched in any courthouse. It has to be asked, recorded, and written down, while the people who remember are still here to tell it.

About this record: 998 people across 9 generations were drawn from the 1985 book and later research; Landon's direct line was added in 2026 from family records. Living relatives appear by name only — their dates, places, and details are kept private. Where the book contradicts itself or a record looks doubtful, the person's page carries a research note rather than a silent correction.

About This Project

The Bell Family History
by Mary Wiltse Heck — September 1985
104 pages of letters, courthouse records, wills, maps, and photographs, gathered over eight years.

This website exists because of one person's patience. In the late 1970s Mary Wiltse Heck, a great-granddaughter of James Hamilton and Mary Elizabeth Bell, set out to write down everything the family remembered before it was lost. She wrote letters, visited courthouses across Ohio and Iowa, and interviewed the oldest Bells then living. The 104-page book she finished in 1985 is the source of nearly everyone and everything you'll find here.

The 2026 Research

In 2026 the book was carefully digitized — every page transcribed, every person and family entered into a structured record, and a handful of details verified against primary sources: federal land patents, census records, and death certificates. That research also restored one branch the 1985 book had missed — the line of Curtis Keith Bell, confirmed through his death certificate and added to the family's tenth-child branch from West Bend, Iowa. The tree now runs a full nine generations.

How to Read the Tree

Each person's page shows what the book and records say — birth, death, marriage, children, and the sources behind them. A ★ marks the direct line down to the present day. Where sources disagree, you'll see a research note instead of a quiet guess.

A note on privacy. This is a public page, so living relatives are shown by name only — no dates, places, or personal details. The complete records, and a GEDCOM file for genealogy software, are kept privately within the family.

The Part That Isn't Written Yet

The book ends in 1985. The generations since — and the stories the oldest relatives still carry — are the missing final chapter, and the one part no courthouse can supply. If you're family and you remember a name, a farm, a photograph, or a story, that's exactly what belongs here next.
